02 — Cross-cutting findings

What recurs across cases

Coverage

Coverage reflects the inherited housing regime and the legal tools available to change it. Without the power to produce, acquire, or reserve stock, and without stable funding streams, supply cannot keep pace with demand, regardless of municipal effort.

Accessibility

Accessibility depends as much on market regulation as on direct subsidy. Barcelona's combination of demand-side support with supply-side rules tempers displacement in specific sub-markets in a way that neither Turin's targeted funds nor the nationally mediated action of Athens or Lisbon can match.

Inclusion

Inclusion depends on whether housing policy is co-produced with civil society under stable institutional arrangements. In Athens, solidarity networks frequently substitute for missing state capacity; in Berlin, civil society itself drafts the law the administration resists passing.

03 — The state as obstacle, not only as enabler

Two timelines of the national-local pendulum

The Berlin and Lisbon cases show how that pendulum swings in both directions within a single legislative cycle.

state decision or restriction municipal response or mandate
Berlin — disputed legislative competence
Feb 2020
The state of Berlin passes the Mietendeckel, its own rent cap, claiming regional competence over housing.
Mar 2021
The Federal Constitutional Court annuls the Mietendeckel: rent regulation for private housing falls under exclusive federal competence.
Sep 2021
The Deutsche Wohnen & Co. enteignen referendum: 59.1% vote to socialize the stock of large corporate landlords. Non-binding.
2023–2025
An expert commission confirms that socialization is legal; the Senate delays the framework law. A federal court also strikes down the municipal right of first refusal.
2026
A second, binding referendum, timed to coincide with the state elections; campaigners draft their own bill.
Lisbon — the short-term rental and Golden Visa pendulum
Oct 2023
Portugal drops real estate purchase as a route to the Golden Visa, within the "Mais Habitação" package.
2023
The same package freezes new short-term rental licences nationwide in high-density municipalities.
Nov 2024
A new government reverses the freeze (Decree-Law 76/2024): licences become permanent and transferable again.
Dec 2025
Lisbon passes its own containment ordinance (RMAL): blocks new licences where density exceeds 10%, with some parishes reaching 66.9%.
2026
Despite years of regulatory back-and-forth, rents keep climbing: municipal containment arrives after the pressure has already built up.
04 — Recommendations for city networks

From comparative evidence to an advocacy agenda

01

Twin track: expand supply and regulate the market

Supply alone, as Turin shows, stabilizes households but does not reprice the market. It must be paired with rent regulation, short-term rental governance, and eviction prevention.

02

Push for legal authority at the national level

Golden Visa schemes and permissive short-term rental regimes should be treated as housing issues, not just investment or tourism policy. Athens and Lisbon show that local action alone cannot offset these distortions.

03

Secure a regulatory toolkit, not only subsidies

Land use, leasing, short-term rental control, and value capture. Barcelona indicates it is this combination, not subsidy size, that drives outcomes.

04

Formalize co-production while recognizing its friction

Partnerships with anti-eviction organizations should expect constructive friction, with transparent grievance channels, rather than a straightforwardly collaborative model.

05

Use inter-city alliances for binding benchmarks

Networks such as the Mayors for Housing Alliance add the most value when tied to EU funding conditions, rather than relying on non-binding declarations.
